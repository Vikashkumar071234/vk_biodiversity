Introduction to Biodiversity

The term biodiversity is made up of two words - bio and diversity. Bio means living and diversity means variety. So the variety or variability of organisms and ecosystem is reffered to as biodiversity. The existence of millions of plants, animals and microorgnaisms,their genetic background and the complex ecosystem to which they belong show the immense biodiversity present in biosphere.

The term biodiversity was coined in 1985. It is important in natural as well as artificial ecosystems. It deals with nature's variety, the biosphere.

Biodiversity holds ecological and economic significance. It provides us with nourishment, housing, fuel, clothing and several other resources. It also extracts monetary benefits through tourism. Therefore, it is very important to have a good knowledge of biodiversity for a sustainable livelihood.

If an alien from a distant galaxy were to visit our planet Earth, the first thing that would amaze and baffle him would most probably be the enormous diversity of life that he would encounter. Even for humans, the rich variety of living organisms with which they share this planet never ceases to astonish and fascinate us. The common man would find it hard to believe that there are more than 20,000 species of ants, 3,00,000 species of beetles, 28,000 species of fishes and nearly 20,000 species of orchids.

Kinds of biodiversity

Genetic biodiversity

Genetic diversity refers to the variety of genetic information within a particular species or population. It is the result of mutations, genetic recombination, and other mechanisms that generate new genetic variations. Genetic diversity is essential for the survival and adaptation of a species to changing environmental conditions, as it provides the raw material for natural selection to act upon.

Species Diversity

Species diversity refers to the variety of different species that exist within a particular area or ecosystem. It encompasses both the number of species present and the relative abundance of each species. A high level of species diversity indicates that an ecosystem has a large number of different species, which can provide a range of ecological services such as pollination, nutrient cycling, and pest control.

Ecosystem Diversity

Ecosystem diversity refers to the variety of different ecosystems that exist within a particular region or across the globe. It includes the range of terrestrial, freshwater, and marine ecosystems, as well as their physical and biological components, such as soils, water, plants, animals, and microorganisms.Ecosystem diversity is important because it provides a wide range of ecological services that support human well-being.
